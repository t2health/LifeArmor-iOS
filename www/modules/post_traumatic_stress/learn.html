Post-Traumatic Stress : Learn

What is PTSD?

Post-traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is an anxiety disorder. PTSD is caused by experiencing traumatic events, such as combat, disasters, terrorism, serious accidents, or physical or sexual assault.

PTSD includes 3 types of symptoms:

  1. Re-experiencing or Reliving the Trauma:
    • Intrusive memories or flashbacks that can seem real
    • Nightmares
    • Feeling upset by trauma reminders
  2. Avoidance and Numbing:
    • Avoiding places or people that are reminders of the trauma
    • Isolating from others
    • Feeling numb or empty
  3. Increased Arousal:
    • Feeling on guard
    • Being irritable
    • Trouble sleeping
    • Startling easily
These symptoms may lead to problems in functioning in a number of settings, including social and family life, work, and school.

Understanding Trauma Memories

Traumatic events may be difficult to fit into what is considered normal experience. The mind may "replay" the experience during the day and during sleep (dreaming) in an attempt to manage the "shock" to the system caused by the trauma. However, obtaining meaning and a sense of control over upsetting thoughts can prove difficult.

What Are Trauma Triggers?

While unwanted thoughts or memories seem to occur "out of the blue," such thoughts are usually triggered by an event that serves as a reminder of the trauma.

Reminders of triggers include sights, sounds, smells, body sensations, and internal experiences (thoughts, memories, feelings).

What Is The Impact Of Trauma Triggers?

It is common for the mind to link trauma events with subsequent day-to-day living. For example, in a combat situation a Marine who was ambushed while driving under a bridge may be "on guard" back home when approaching a highway overpass. While there is no actual threat back home, the mind has learned to link bridges with danger. Back home, bridges come to "trigger" fear or tension, or a keyed-up reaction.

When the mind "pairs" highway bridges with danger in a combat environment, the mind is correctly adapting to circumstances. When approaching an overpass in a combat setting, the warfighter should be keyed up and ready for danger. Back home, this "overlearned" response, adaptive in one setting, is no longer helpful. Unfortunately, being triggered by harmless situations also creates stress for the mind and body.

Triggers have another unfortunate result - individuals begin to avoid the things or places or people that remind them of the trauma. In the above example, the Marine goes out of his way to avoid highway overpasses. Such avoidance has additional costs by adding additional time to travel and by potentially causing social embarrassment when explaining to passengers the reason for detouring around overpasses.

Back home, the mind must "re-learn" that highway bridges are a common and safe means to travel from one location to another. In other words, the mind must "disconnect" now-useless reminders from the brain's danger circuits by recognizing trauma triggers and responding to them in new ways.

Identifying Your Trauma Triggers

Some trauma triggers are easy to identify because they clearly remind you of the trauma. Other triggers may be less easy to recognize. Learning to identify your triggers has benefits: you'll feel more in control, and you'll be better prepared to face future situations.

Maintaining a "Trigger Record" is a great way to identify triggers (especially the ones that are less obvious). A Trigger Record will help you recognize your triggers, and allow you to monitor your improved reactions to those triggers.

The Trigger Record

You can find printable trigger records in afterdeployment.org's Post-Traumatic Stress library. Or, you can simply write down your observations whenever you have experienced a trauma trigger:

First, record the date and time.
Example: Monday, 2 p.m.

Second, describe the trigger.
Example: Driving under an overpass.

Identify the emotions you were feeling.
Example: Fear and worry.

Identify your body's reactions.
Example: Started sweating and felt nauseous.

Record your thoughts and what you were telling yourself.
Example: I'm in danger. The overpass might blow up.

Identify the ways that the trigger differed from the original trauma
Example: I'm home, not in a combat situation.

On a scale from 1 to 10 with 1 being minimal and 10 being extreme, rate the amount of difficulty you had managing the trigger.
Example: 7.

Use the Trigger Record as soon as possible each time you are triggered. If you aren't sure what triggered you, start by describing your thoughts, or your feelings or some sensation that you are having, and what you remember about the situation. Then, work backwards to determine the trigger that set you off.

The RID Tool

The RID Tool is a three-step skill. Use it when you are triggered to help "RID" yourself of reactions to triggers.

STEP 1: Relax
STEP 2: Identify the Trigger
STEP 3: Decide Your Response

  • Step 1: Relax. Here are some ways to relax when you have been triggered:
    • Take a few deep breaths.
    • Tell yourself something calming: "I'm not in danger now." "The trauma is not happening again."
    • Get a drink of water or walk to the restroom and splash cool water on your face.
  • Step 2: Identify the Trigger. Once you are relaxed, try identifying what factors may have caused the trigger.
    • Ask yourself, "What is triggering me?" "How is this situation like the actual trauma trigger? By recognizing that the current trigger is different from the actual trauma, you'll begin to see that you are not in danger.
    • If you can't identify the trigger, don't worry. Work on relaxing and calming yourself.
  • Step 3: Decide Your Response. Deciding how to respond to a situation can help you feel more in control. Try these suggestions:
    • Go back to what you were doing before the trigger.
    • Start a conversation with someone around you.
    • Seek support from someone you trust.
    • Focus on sights and sounds around you.
    • Find a positive distraction that will hold your attention.

RID Tool: Deciding to Stay in the Trigger Situation

Triggers And Fear:

If your trigger makes you feel fearful or anxious, your first impulse may be to leave the situation. Don't give in to the impulse to take flight or avoid; instead, stay in the situation and face your fear. Don't leave the situation until you feel the trigger is less powerful than when you first confronted it.

If you run from the trigger, you won't learn that the trigger is not dangerous. Avoiding your triggers may ease your anxiety in the short term, but will create bad habits and make overcoming the trigger's hold on you more and more difficult. Gradually, 'staring down' a trigger will weaken it; the trigger will hold less power over you, and you will feel less distress in the presence of it.

Triggers And Anger:

If the trigger makes you angry and you believe staying in the situation may cause you to act in a way you'll regret, remove yourself from the situation and cool off. Try some relaxation tools; or find someone you trust and talk things out.

What To Do After A Trigger Event Is Over

  • Don't judge or criticize yourself harshly about how you handled the trigger.
  • Treat yourself like a loved one or a close friend who might be going through the same thing.
  • Regardless of outcome, see the trigger as a learning experience.
  • Complete a trigger record, and review what went well and how to improve your responses to the trigger.
  • Talk about the trigger event with a close friend or loved one.
  • Remind yourself that, with time and practice, triggers get easier to handle.
  • Reward yourself for successfully managing your trigger.

The PLAN Tool

Here's a simple strategy to PLAN for encountering triggers or other stressful situations:
  • STEP 1: Prepare for the situation. Think about ways to manage the situation. Discuss the situation with someone whose judgment you respect. Ask yourself:
    • Can I relax before the situation?
    • Would it help to take along a friend who knows what I might experience?
    • Can I change the situation, or make a plan to make it easier to deal with?
  • STEP 2: Let go of your worry. Worrying how you will react to a situation can be worse than the actual situation that's causing the worry! To let go of your worry:
    • Ask yourself, "What exactly am I worried about?"
    • Ask yourself, "Are my fears reasonable?"
    • Rehearse ways to successfully manage the situation in your mind.
    • Tell yourself, "I've prepared for the situation and that's the best I can do."
    • If you start to worry, focus on a positive thought or activity.
  • STEP 3: Accept that you may experience distress but that you can manage your reaction. Even normal stress can become more difficult to manage after a trauma. Accepting that you will experience negative feelings can help you because:
    • While accepting does not mean feeling good about being upset, it does mean that you have realistic expectations about your reactions.
    • It can help you to cope with situations better.
    • You won't allow negative feelings to control your life.
  • STEP 4: Note all of your coping skills and helpful resources. Think of all the skills you have learned for managing stressful situations. Remind yourself: "During stressful situations, I can…"
    • Have a friend or loved one with me.
    • Practice relaxation exercises.
    • Say positive things to myself.
    • Use positive strategies that worked on my triggers in the past.
