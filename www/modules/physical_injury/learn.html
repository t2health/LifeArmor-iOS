Physical Injury : Learn

Physical Injuries And Deployment

During deployment you may have suffered a serious physical injury. Your physical injury may have caused disfigurement, serious impairment of health or serious loss or impairment of the function of any bodily organ. Coping with the aftermath of a physical injury can be challenging for you and your loved ones. It's important to remember to be patient with yourself. Experiencing a wide range of emotions is a natural and important part of the healing process. The way you adapt to your injury will be influenced by:

  • Your personality.
  • Your life experiences.
  • Your health before the injury.
  • Support from your family and friends.
  • The meaning you assign to the event that caused your injury.

The Importance Of Acceptance

While coping with an injury is a personal process, some aspects of healing after a deployment-related injury are common for everyone. For example, accepting a serious injury means dealing with loss. This loss can include: body parts or functions; the loss of physical capabilities and skills; the loss of a former identity; the loss of mobility; and perhaps the loss of your future plans.

The length of your grieving process largely depends on:

  • Your personality.
  • Your life experiences.
  • Your health before the injury.
  • Support from your family and friends.
  • The meaning you assign to the event that caused your injury.

Keep in mind that everyone has a different recovery timeframe. Taking longer to reach the acceptance period than someone else doesn't mean you're weak.

Seven Suggestions On Adjusting To Your Injury

  1. Focus on what you still have and what you can still do.
  2. Nurture your sense of humor. Be prepared for stares and insensitive comments.
  3. Set goals for yourself. Take it one day at a time or even one hour at a time.
  4. Seek balance. Don't isolate yourself and make time to work, rest, and play.
  5. Do not let others dictate your mood or actions.
  6. Be assertive.
  7. Never give up. Each day may pose a new challenge and you may struggle but as long as you are alive anything is possible.

At some point in the various journeys we embark on in our lives, we get to a part where we feel like giving up. Sometimes we give up before we even start and other times we give up just before we are about to make it over that hurdle. Keep in mind you have the power to choose how you want to see yourself and your situation. You have the power to think positively, negatively, or realistically. You choose your mood, attitude, and feelings. You control your own thoughts, feelings, and behavior.

Your Treatment Team

Beating the challenges posed by your injuries takes attitude, determination, and practice. You'll need to rely on strengths you've learned in the past and incorporate new strategies. Sports teams need a coach to teach new skills, monitor progress, and encourage development. You can benefit from the help of a coach, too. Your "coach" is called a therapist. In fact, you may have a whole roster of therapists/coaches aside from your medical providers:

  • A physical therapist assists with proper movement and healing to improve physical performance.
  • An occupational therapist helps you adjust and make changes so that you are able to manage your daily needs yourself.
  • A mental health therapist will help you make adjustments to changes in your life.
  • A spiritual therapist is someone of your faith or belief who can help you understand any conflicts between your beliefs and your experiences.

Accept help, but realize that, ultimately, it's your attitude, practice, and performance that will determine how things turn out.

Change Your Self-Image

If your deployment injuries left you disabled or disfigured, you might compare yourself to people around you. You may compare the way you look and function now to the way you looked and functioned before your injury. These comparisons are normal and natural. But, in order to recover and thrive, you need to develop a new self-image apart from those comparisons. Developing a positive self-image after an injury may take a while, so be patient and focus on the following:

  • Give up your old, pre-injury self-image. As a service member trained to stay in shape, you may base your self-esteem on your physical appearance and abilities. Your physical self is just one aspect of your total self. These two exercises can help you keep your physical appearance and abilities in perspective.
    • Accept your new body (including a prosthetic if you have an amputation). To fully recover, you need to visualize and accept your appearance with the injury.
    • Combat the stereotypes of "physical perfection." When you show no self-consciousness in front of others, you educate others and help them overcome our culture's misguided emphasis on "physical perfection."
  • Make sense of your injury. One of the toughest jobs related to adjusting to war injuries is to make sense out of the injury and why it happened. Service members who find positive meaning in their sacrifice and fit their injury into their world view have a greater sense of well-being and less anxiety and depression.
    • Search for a positive direction that you could pursue because of your injury. Have you learned to appreciate small things you used to take for granted? Have you formed new relationships or grown closer to loved ones? Has the injury increased your wisdom or faith? Have you learned new skills you didn't have before? Are you stronger and more resilient than you ever thought you could be?
    • Look to your faith to help you find meaning.
    • Find a new purpose for your life in light of your injury. This might be an immediate purpose, like turning an ordinary event into something special and significant. Or it might be a long-term purpose, like helping other injured people get their lives back on track.
  • Set Goals For The Future. Once you accept and make sense of your injury, you can start to see your life in terms of possibilities, instead of limitations. Begin to set goals for the future. Goal-setting is important for both your physical recovery and for positive mental health. Setting goals will give you a sense of purpose and help you focus on your future. Here are some steps to goal-setting that will help you plan for a positive future:
    • Write out your goal: "My goal is to ..."
    • Make sure the goal is yours, not someone else's. Don't set goals based on what you think society expects you to do.
    • Make your goal as specific as possible. For example, "my goal is to start hiking again," is too general. Be specific: "My goal is to climb Mt. Whitney."
    • Write down all the intermediate steps required to accomplish your goal.
  • Handle Unwanted Attention. Visible injuries, burns and amputations, attract more attention and wheelchairs will also indicate an injury or disability. Here are some tips for coping with common challenges.
    • When someone stares, accept it as part of human nature. People naturally pay attention to anything out of the ordinary, so your injuries may draw attention. People will stare out of curiosity, compassion, or concern - not rudeness. You may not be able to control their staring but you can control your reaction. Don't allow other people's reactions to prevent you from living a good life.
    • When someone is curious about your injury have an answer ready. This will reduce any embarrassment or anger you might feel. Rehearse your response until it feels natural and friendly (not hostile). If someone asks for more information and you don't feel like talking about it, have a response ready for that, too: "That's all I care to say about it today. I'm sure you understand."
    • When someone makes fun of your injury you may feel the urge to get revenge or defend yourself. But either response will fuel the other person's wishes. Instead, your response should: stop the other person from harassing you, and maintain your self-respect. Ignore put downs, or if you feel the need to respond to an insult, use humor to diffuse the situation.
  • Be Confident And Friendly. Whenever you are out in public or enter a new social situation, you can influence the way people respond to you by projecting confidence. When people see you are self-assured and friendly, they will focus on your personality instead of your injuries. Use The STEPS tool to accomplish this. Practice the following steps until they become natural and automatic.
    • S = Self-talk (make it positive) - "I accept myself and I'm comfortable with other people."
    • T = Tone of voice - friendly, warm, enthusiastic.
    • E = Eye contact - look people in the eye.
    • P = Posture - head raised, shoulders back.
    • S = Smile - confident but approachable.
  • Ask For Help. There may be times when you need help. It's important to ask for help in a direct manner so people can figure out what you need. Direct requests have three parts.
    • State your needs: "I need to get to the doctor's office tomorrow."
    • Make the request: "Would you be able to drive me?"
    • Acknowledge the other person's response: "Thank you, I really appreciate it." Exercise: Make a list of areas where you need help. Then write a script with a direct request for each item, using the three-part request approach listed above. Finally, practice each request until it becomes automatic. The next time you ask for help, you'll be able to ask in a direct, confident, and open manner.
  • Develop New Relationships. Relationships keep you from becoming withdrawn or isolated. Friends, family and coworkers can sustain you through your physical recovery. Their support will make your daily triumphs more meaningful. You may meet new friends in the hospital, at support groups, and in recreational or community activities so as you build new, remember:
    • Be a good listener. Instead of planning what to say while the other person is speaking, listen carefully. Every once in a while, repeat what they're saying in your own words. Use phrases like "What I hear you saying is…", or "You seem to be saying…Is that right?" This helps the other person feel understood.
    • Ask questions that show you are interested in the whole person, not just their work or their deployment experience. Everyone has a unique personal story to tell if you act interested. If you follow these guidelines, people will know you are comfortable with your injury and interested in getting to know them.

Returning To Work After An Injury

Finding or returning to employment is one of the most important aspects of adjusting to an injury. Employment will increase your self-esteem, personal independence, and financial security. Ask yourself these questions when looking for employment.

  • Are you capable of returning to the job you formerly held? If not, are you able to work in a similar position where your expertise can be valuable?
  • Would you prefer to work in a different field from your former employment? If so, what training or education will you need? Is it realistic to pursue this training or education?
  • Does the new job have advancement potential? Does it offer training? Is there room for growth?
  • Are you interested in this field? Or is this "just a job"?

You will perform better and find more satisfaction in a job that really "calls" to you. People with disabilities cannot be denied employment just because they're disabled if they are able to perform the essential duties of the position. For more information on employment and disability, visit the U.S. Department of Labor, Office of Disability Employment Policy Website at http://www.dol.gov/odep/pubs/fact/laws.htm

Compensation Issues

As an injured service member or veteran, you may qualify for compensation. To find out more about veterans compensation, visit http://www.cms.hhs.gov/apps/firststep/content/vacomp-qa.html

If you left a civilian job to serve your country, you qualify for compensation from the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act of 1994 (USERRA). To find out more about USERRA, check out http://www.dol.gov/compliance/laws/comp-userra.htm You can also visit Military.com website at http://www.military.com/benefits/military-pay/va-disability-compensation and the Disabilities Programs webpage of the Social Security Administration at: http://www.ssa.gov/disability/

Get Involved In Your Community

Through your service to your country, you gained life experience. One way of sharing that experience with others is by working or volunteering in your community

  • Attend town meetings and... Vote. Most communities hold open public meetings at least once a year. These meetings are excellent places to learn about the issues and allow you to voice your concerns. Vote in all local and national elections. Contact your local town hall for registration and voting information.
  • Volunteer your skills. There are many organizations that could use your skills and assistance, to find volunteer opportunities in your area, check out Volunteer Match at http://www.volunteermatch.org.
  • Be a mentor. Mentoring is a great way to impact a child's life. Consider becoming a Big Brother or Big Sister. To find out more, visit http://www.bigbrothersbigsisters.org.
  • Participate in a sports activity. Check with your community recreation department to find sporting activities and youth leagues in your area. Or consider coaching or being an assistant coach to a local team.
  • Get involved with your child's school. If you have children, join the Parent Teacher Association (PTA). This is an excellent way to learn more about your child's school activities.
  • Get involved with your local faith-based organization. Your local church, synagogue, or spiritual center, will provide opportunities such as education classes, singing, fundraising, or teaching.
  • Participate in a community garden. Gardening with others offers exercise, recreation, companionship, and the opportunity to add beauty to your community. To find community gardens near you, contact the American Community Gardening Association at http://www.communitygarden.org.

Find Recreational Activities

Physical activity reduces stress, increases muscle and cardiovascular tone, and may increase the release of endorphins - the brain's natural pain killers. Thanks to modern advancements in prosthetics, mechanical aides, specialized sports equipment, and access laws, recreational options are ever evolving and limitless. Recreational activities also enhance self-confidence, increase independent functioning, and develop strength and endurance. If you're looking for a new adventure, try one of the following:

  • Get in touch with the great outdoors. Try fishing, mountain climbing, white water rafting, horseback riding, canoeing or kayaking, snowshoe, snowboarding or skiing.
  • Try a new intramural sport offered at your local recreational center. Some activities offered can include ballroom dancing classes, softball, basketball, soccer, kickball, dodge ball, tennis, or join a swim team or try your hand at golf.
  • If you prefer cold weather, look for local ski or snowboard trips. You may also enjoy snowmobiling, snowshoeing, cross country skiing, ice skating or sledding.
  • If you're seeking a bit of an adrenaline rush you can always try paragliding, skydiving or maybe enjoy a hot air balloon ride.

For more suggestions, check out the following websites:

  • The Disabled Sports USA website at: http://www.dsusa.org/about-overview.html
  • Military inStep at: http://www.amputee-coalition.org/military-instep/sports-recreation-resources.html
  • The Sports and Recreation webpage of the Paralyzed Veterans Association at: http://www.pva.org/site/PageServer?pagename=sports_main.

Managing Your Pain With Medications

  • Narcotics: Also known as opioid based drugs, morphine, oxycodone, methadone and fentanyl are used for acute pain, such as pain after an amputation or burn surgery. Also, these narcotics are often used for chronic pain in low doses if other methods are not effective but keep in mind they are habit forming and should be used as prescribed by a medical professional.
  • Anti-inflammatory Drugs: Aspirin and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen (advil and motrin) and naproxen (aleve) reduce swelling, irritation and pain. When treating more serious inflammatory conditions one can be prescribed steroidal drugs like cortisone or prednisone.
  • Antidepressants: Newer medications (like Cymbalta) are used for chronic pain or pain due to nerve damage or an amputation. Antidepressants may also induce restful sleep.
  • Anti-seizure Medications: May relieve what some patients describe as "shooting" or "burning" pain caused by damaged nerves and aching pain. Additionally, these medications are used for chronic pain.
  • Injections: Local anesthetics (such as Novocain) can be injected around nerve roots and directly into muscles or joints. Injections reduce swelling, muscle spasms and abnormal nerve activity.

Managing Your Pain With Hot And Cold Compresses

When seeking pain relief through hot and cold compresses it can be beneficial to experiment and see which treatment works best for you. (Follow your health care provider's directions on how long to apply each.)

  • Heat Compresses:
    • Heat above 104F blocks the body's ability to detect pain.
    • An electric heating pad or hot water bottle applied to the site of the pain may give relief and will reduce the joint and muscle stiffness.
    • Heat increases the blood flow to the injured area, bringing nutrients to damaged tissues.
    • NOTE: Heat should not be applied immediately after an injury.
  • Cold Compresses:
    • Relieves pain by numbing the affected area.
    • Reduces swelling and inflammation.
    • Reduces bleeding.

Managing Your Pain With Acupuncture/Acupressure/Massage

  • Acupuncture: Is an alternative medicine that treats patients by insertion and manipulation of needles into the body. Acupuncture is approved by the National Institutes of Health for the treatment of chronic pain and may help with "phantom" limb pain.
  • Acupressure: Is also an alternative medicine that involves placing physical pressure by hand, elbow, or with the aid of various devices, on different pressure points on the surface of the body to bring about relief through greater balance and circulation of fluids.
  • Massage: Is the manipulation of superficial and deeper layers of muscle and connective tissue to enhance function, aid in the healing process and promote relaxation and well-being. Therapeutic massage may disrupt the transmission of pain signals to the brain and stimulate the release of the body's natural painkillers (endorphins).

Managing Your Pain With Electrical Stimulation

  • Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS): A low-voltage electrical current is transmitted through electrodes that are taped to the skin near the pain. The electric current may prevent pain signals from reaching the brain. It can be effective with many types of pain, particularly phantom limb pain.

Managing Your Pain With Hypnosis

  • Hypnotherapy: Modern hypnotherapy can leave a person in a deep relaxation and leaves the mind open to suggestions of pain relief. Approved by the National Institutes of Health as a pain control method, hypnotherapy may be useful in reducing both acute and chronic pain and anxiety associated with painful procedures (i.e., burn treatments).
  • Autohypnosis: Is a self-induced hypnosis (or deep relaxation) and can be used to create a state of relaxation or distraction from pain.

Managing Your Pain With Stress Reduction Techniques

Stress intensifies both chronic and acute pain. One way to manage your stress and potentially reduce your pain can be practicing deep breathing exercises (check out Breath2Relax, a mobile application for your iPhone or iPad, provides simple instructions for performing breathing exercises proven to decrease the body's stress response). Another technique is progressive muscle relaxation. This exercise involves tensing and relaxing muscle groups throughout the body and releasing the muscle tension associated with increased pain.

Avoid The Following When Managing Your Pain

  • Don't self-medicate the pain by overusing alcohol or abusing drugs.
  • Don't try to "tough out" the pain. Pain is treatable.
  • Let your doctor know if you have new or constant pain. Be open to trying different treatments or combinations of treatments until you find what works for you.
  • Don't use tobacco products if you have a spinal injury. Tobacco can lead to degenerative disk disease, which causes back and leg pain.
